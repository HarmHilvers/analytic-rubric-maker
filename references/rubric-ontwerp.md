# Rubric Design

Use this reference when writing level descriptors for an analytic rubric.

## Core Rules

- Anchor `good` to the criterion itself.
- Make each criterion observable and assessable before writing level descriptors.
- Keep descriptors short.
- Let level differences emerge from qualitative features of the work or behavior.
- Always follow the user's language in the visible output.

## Level Anchors

Use these anchors as design heuristics, not as fixed templates.

### Good

Treat `good` as the performance that matches the criterion. Describe what must be demonstrably present when the student actually meets the criterion.

Think in terms of features such as:

- appropriate justification
- coherent approach or reasoning
- adequate use of relevant theory or sources
- visible command of the required task
- appropriate independence within the context

### Sufficient

Treat `sufficient` as performance that meets the standard in broad terms but remains limited in quality or scope.

Think in terms of features such as:

- limited justification
- correct but weakly integrated
- single perspective
- descriptive or procedural rather than genuinely analytical
- more guidance needed
- little transfer to other contexts

### Excellent

Treat `excellent` as performance that demonstrably exceeds `good` through relevant added value.

Think in terms of features such as:

- greater depth or sharpness
- stronger coherence and integration
- critical and purposeful use of theory or sources
- well-judged choices in a more complex context
- transfer to new or different situations
- strong practical usefulness
- original or innovative approach, when appropriate to the criterion

Do not make `excellent` depend automatically on originality. Use originality only when the criterion or task calls for it.

### Insufficient

Treat `insufficient` as performance in which essential parts are missing or not demonstrable.

Think in terms of features such as:

- missing
- incorrect
- incomplete
- internally inconsistent
- not traceable
- unsupported
- not aligned with the task

## Rewriting Criteria

Rewrite rough criteria using these moves:

- replace vague words with observable behavior or visible product features
- make clear what is being assessed: content, approach, reasoning, justification, reflection, execution, or communication
- name only relevant quality dimensions
- keep each criterion as narrow as possible without losing meaning

Example:

- too vague: `uses theory well`
- better: `justifies choices with appropriate theory and applies it correctly`

## Differences Between Levels

Use quality differences such as:

- completeness
- accuracy
- coherence
- depth
- precision
- independence
- transfer
- breadth of perspective
- practical usefulness
- handling of complexity

Do not let levels differ only through loose scale words.

## Avoid These Patterns

Avoid descriptors such as:

- `somewhat clear`, `clear`, `very clear`
- `sufficient`, `good`, `very good`
- `reasonably justified`, `well justified`, `very well justified`

These formulations show too little observable difference.

## Delivery Format

Prefer a markdown table with these columns:

| Criterion | Insufficient | Sufficient | Good | Excellent |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Name the learning outcome above the table. Show rewritten criteria separately only when that improves readability.
